## How to prepare for your first Directive 8020 single player run

A good first run starts before chapter one. Because this is a consequence-focused title, prep is less about hardware and more about attention habits.

### 1) Pick your run philosophy before you start
Choose one of these styles and stick to it for the full campaign:

| Run Style | Core Rule | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Roleplay Purist** | Make choices as your character would | Most immersive first run |
| **Survival Optimizer** | Prioritize keeping as many alive as possible | Players who want maximum roster outcomes |
| **Risk & Truth Hunter** | Push dangerous options to uncover hidden context | Lore-focused players |

Switching styles mid-run creates inconsistent decision logic, which can produce messy outcomes that feel accidental.

### 2) Set practical session conditions
Narrative horror depends on pacing. Distractions weaken your decision quality.

| Setting | Recommended Setup | Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| **Audio** | Headphones, moderate volume | Better tension cues and environmental detail |
| **Session length** | 90–150 minutes | Keeps story continuity strong |
| **Notifications** | Off/Do Not Disturb | Reduces missed prompts and emotional breaks |
| **Lighting** | Low ambient light | Boosts immersion without eye strain |

### 3) Use a simple decision log
Keep notes after major chapters:
- Who you trusted
- Which risks you took
- Which clues felt unresolved
- Which relationship seems unstable

You don’t need a giant spreadsheet. A short log helps you avoid repeating the same logic on replay.

> **💡 Tip:** After every major turning point, pause for 20 seconds and ask: “Did I choose for short-term safety or long-term information?”

## Choice architecture: making better decisions under pressure

Many players lose characters not because they’re careless, but because they panic into binary thinking. The best approach is triage: people, information, and exposure risk.

### Decision matrix for high-stress moments

| Situation Type | Default Instinct | Better Solo Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediate threat** | Sprint or react fast | Identify who is most vulnerable first |
| **Trust conflict** | Back favorite character | Cross-check motive + recent behavior |
| **Unknown corridor/objective** | Explore everything now | Secure safe route before deep exploration |
| **Split group choice** | Keep team together | Split only if objective urgency is clear |
| **Moral dilemma** | Save nearest person | Consider downstream impact on group stability |

In **Directive 8020 single player**, pressure scenes likely reward composure over speed. If the game follows the studio’s known style, your “right” choice may not be obvious in the moment—and that is the point.

### Three-step crisis method
Use this in every major branch:

1. **Identify the core risk**  
   Is this a threat to life, trust, or knowledge?
2. **Select one priority**  
   You usually cannot maximize all three at once.
3. **Accept the tradeoff**  
   Own the consequence and move forward confidently.

This prevents “choice regret spirals,” where you second-guess everything and lose narrative momentum.

## Common mistakes solo players make (and how to avoid them)

Even experienced narrative players fall into these traps:

### Mistake 1: “Main character bias”
You overprotect one person and underinvest in team dynamics.

**Fix:** Treat each character as a system node, not just a personality favorite.

### Mistake 2: “Lore greed”
You chase every mystery immediately, even when threat levels are high.

**Fix:** Use staged exploration. Stabilize your position, then investigate.

### Mistake 3: “Panic confirmation”
You trust the first plausible explanation and ignore contradicting clues.

**Fix:** Keep two active theories at all times until evidence resolves conflict.

### Mistake 4: “Reload addiction”
You undo every painful outcome and flatten the emotional stakes.

**Fix:** For your first **Directive 8020 single player** run, commit to no rewinds unless technical issues force one.
